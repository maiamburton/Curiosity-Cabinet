Welcome to the Curiosity Cabinet!

Hi! My name is Maia Burton. I'm a computer science graduate from UC Davis with a range of interests. Beyond my major, I like folding origami, reading murder mysteries, and learning new skills.

I'm a bit of a hoarder/pack rat when it comes to hobbies and unique, unusual websites. This site is my personal collection of interests and experiences—a mix of what I know and what I'm curious about. Maybe you'll discover a new interest too.

Books

The Broken Window is the 9th book in the Lincoln Rhyme series, but was the one I started with, not realizing it was a series at the time. Not only does Jeffery Deaver write incredible plot twists and a fast-pasted narrative, this novel in particular explores the terrifying side of technology and data collecting. The criminal in this book steals identities, profiles their victims, and falsifies government documents to misdirect authorities and frame others for murders they committed.

I've read five of the sixteen books in this series so far, and each one has had an incredible reveal and a well-researched, unique theme throughout. I also love the fact that I can speculate as much as I want, who the killer is, and still never see the plot twists coming... whether that reveals more about my detective skills or Deaver's writing ones is up for debate. You can decide.

  • 48 Laws of Power by Robert Greene
    • While this book receives a lot of criticism for it's tutorial-like phrasing how to manipulate and gain power over others, it can be a great source for people to better recognize and handle when they are being taken advantage of. It's full of captivating history showing these laws in practice and resulting transgressions against them too. Everyone needs to feel in control of their lives, and power doesn't determine someone's morals. Their morals determine how their power is used.
  • Thinking in Bets by Anne Duke
    • Poker and chess are well known strategy games, but what makes poker a more useful skill, at least in my opinion and the author's, is that poker is based on imperfect information—players don't have all the facts. Unlike chess, poker allows for players to bluff and win despite a bad hand. In life, we rarely have all the information before making a big decision; it's important to make the best one with what you have, and that's what Anne Duke teaches in her book.
  • Atomic Habits by James Clear:
    • Both my parents recently read and love this book. They recommend it to everyone they speak with. While I have not read the book, I did love the summary of it I found on Youtube a few years ago. Atomic Habits covers habit building methods such as:
    • Stacking and Starting: using a current habit for your new behavior to piggy back on
    • Having a starting rule: counting a two-minute goal often jumpstarts your motivation to continue
    • Syncing and Scoring: pairing experiences you crave with the new habit you dread doing
